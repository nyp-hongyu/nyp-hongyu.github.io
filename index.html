The snare drum is the most basic part of the drum, it is the part you will learn to play first, starting with your single stroke rolls. It makes a crisp and notable sound, caused by pressing many beads at the bottom of it via a wire that gets tightened to press it up, somewhere between hitting sand, an empty metal pot, and paper. It also has a switch at the side to "off" it which will take it back to sounding like the toms.

The high tom produces a much higher pitched sound than any other drum, like hitting an empty plastic bottle. Seen as much as the floor tom but less used as a boom and more used for its nice sound.

Less seen compared to the high tom and floor tom, it's at the top right and is really mostly used in drum fills and solos.

A commonly used drum in rock songs, pop songs, sometimes even orchestral arrangements. Its big heavy bass sound meshes well when played together with a bass kick drum.

The second drum one will be taught to play. Alternatively known as the bass drum, the kick makes a loud boom when stepping on its pedal, as it forces a hammer/mallet to whack the drum. It's used a lot alongside the snare and hi-hat in Rock songs and way too much for Metal and DnB songs.

Very well known. The crash cymbal is something you've definitely heard before, it is THE cymbal. Placed usually at the beginning of sections or even just bars, it has a fun splash to it that can be played with. Two crash cymbals can be found on most drum sets.

A simple yet vital cymbal, this is the third part a new drummer will likely be taught. It has two parts, the pedal and the cymbal. Between closing and opening the cymbal depending on if you step on the pedal, you will either get a nice tapping noise or sticky metal that hits against each other, unless it opens too much, then you should probably set the default height of it lower.

Commonly used in jazzy, swingy songs, or sometimes blues, the ride cymbal has a nice resounding echo to it even after a light hit. This is used like an alternative hi-hat when you need a different vibe. Hitting the protrusion at the center will cause it to produce a sound akin to a cow-bell.

    The most basic technique. You simply go "Right, Left, Right, Left,..", hitting the drum normally with alternating hands. This is helpful when combined with other techniques just to practice them. Also do practice with this when getting used to higher speeds.


    The Single Stroke Roll, but you alternate every 2 hits. Right right, left left. Also better when combined with other techniques but benefits quite a bit from learning to play this consistently fast


    Playing a Ghost Note on a drum is when you hit the drum much lighter and softer compared to normal. It shouldn't be completely silent, but just soft. It doesn't have to be barely audible either, as long as it is lighter and like a "ghost" compared to the other notes .


    This is played with two sticks. Hold one like normal and the other barely above the drum. Then, use both sticks to hit the drum. Since one is much lower than the other, it will produce a ghost note, while the other is normal. This produces two hits in rapid succession, with the first being just noticeable.


    A bit more advanced than the flam, read that one first. You simply do your light hits twice before the normal stroke. Don't let the stick tap or bounce too much, only two.


    A standard technique. Loosen your grip against your drumsticks so that they can freely pivot while you are holding them. You should be able to swing them just by flicking your wrist. When you can on both, make your stick drop down and bounce onto the snare before catching it, most likely you performed a drag. A roll is simply throwing your hands down with that loose grip to keep a steady flow of fast hits.


    Hold your drumstick, hands should be closer toward the bottom. Fingers curling and ON the stick rather than wrapped around it. Then, rest your wrist against the snare, press down your wrist while raising your fingers and the stick, before slamming your palm down and making a clicking noise on the snare.


    The trickiest technique in this catalog. It's simple to understand but hard to execute, you need to hit the drum and the rim of said drum at the same time. To make it easier, the drum should be hit with the head of the stick, the rounded tip. The neck, the conical section of the stick should strike the rim.
